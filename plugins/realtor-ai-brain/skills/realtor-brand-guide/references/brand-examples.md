# Real Estate Brand Style Sheet Examples

These are real brand style sheets produced for actual real estate agents. Use these as quality benchmarks and design inspiration when building agent brand guides. Study the patterns across all examples to understand what makes a professional real estate brand system.

---

## What these examples teach us

Before diving into individual breakdowns, here are the cross-cutting patterns that appear in every high-quality example:

### Layout & Structure
- **Cover/hero section always leads** with agent photo, name large, market city, and brand values or tagline
- **Sections are clearly labeled** with category headers (Logo Variations, Colors & Fonts, Graphic Elements, Mockups/Applications)
- **Brand applications are shown** — every guide shows the brand applied to real contexts. Note: the brand guide skill delivers a Canva designer brief (DOCX) instead of HTML mockup boxes — the brief contains precise build specs for every template
- **Dark backgrounds dominate hero sections** — light sections are used for content/detail areas
- Multiple logo variations are always shown: primary, horizontal, icon/mark, light version, dark version

### Color Systems
- **3–5 colors maximum** — never more. Most use: 1 dominant dark, 1 primary brand color, 1 accent/metallic, 1 light/white
- **Metallic accents appear frequently** — gold, champagne, bronze — they signal premium positioning
- **Hex codes are always displayed** on swatches — agents need to hand these to designers
- Colors are shown as actual swatches with labels, not just named

### Typography
- **Always two fonts** — one display/headline, one body. Never one, never three
- Font names and weights are explicitly labeled (e.g. "Corsa Grotesk | Black", "Barlow Condensed | Bold")
- A sample alphabet or pangram ("aAbBcCdD 12345") is shown to demonstrate the full character set
- Display fonts tend toward: bold condensed, serif editorial, or geometric sans
- Body fonts tend toward: clean geometric sans, humanist sans

### Brand Voice
- **3–6 adjective words** displayed large, often typographically treated (alternating weights, sizes, or colors)
- Short mission statement: one or two sentences describing who they help and where
- Personality words appear on the cover or in a dedicated brand voice section

### Graphic Elements
- Every brand has 2–3 signature graphic elements beyond color and type: patterns, shapes, textures, lines
- Common elements: dot patterns, abstract organic shapes, geometric polygons, wavy lines, diagonal arrows
- These elements appear consistently across all mockup applications

### Logo Design Patterns
- Agent initials or name-based monogram is the most common logo approach
- Abstract icon (house shape, letter mark, geometric mark) almost always accompanies the wordmark
- Multiple variations shown: stacked, horizontal, icon only, light-on-dark, dark-on-light
- For teams/couples, both individual and combined logos are shown

## Quality Standards Summary

When evaluating a generated brand guide, it should meet all of these:

**Visual completeness:**
- [ ] Cover/hero section with agent name prominent and market city visible
- [ ] Full color palette with hex codes on every swatch
- [ ] Two fonts named, labeled with weights, shown with sample text
- [ ] At least 3 logo variations (primary, icon/mark, alternate background)
- [ ] At least 2–3 graphic elements defined
- [ ] At minimum 2 mockup applications (business card + social post)

**Design quality:**
- [ ] Palette has 3–5 colors maximum — never 6+
- [ ] Colors feel intentional and work together, not randomly selected
- [ ] Font pairing creates clear contrast (not two similar fonts)
- [ ] Dark and light sections alternate — not all one background tone
- [ ] Mockups use real content (agent name, city, actual use case) — not Lorem Ipsum

**Brand identity strength:**
- [ ] Brand voice words are specific to this agent — not generic ("professional, reliable")
- [ ] The visual system would look different from any other agent in the same market
- [ ] Design choices connect to the agent's market, niche, or personality
- [ ] Someone could look at the style sheet and immediately know who this agent is and what they stand for

### New Cross-Cutting Patterns From This Batch

#### Cover/Hero Design Lessons
- **City skyline as background** — Steve Buzogany uses the actual Philadelphia skyline as a full-bleed dark background, then places the agent photo and logo over it. The skyline isn't decorative — it's identity. It immediately says "this person owns this market."
- **Torn paper / texture edge effects** — Kenny Pleasant uses a ripped paper edge between the orange photo background and the dark navy section. Creates visual energy and editorial quality without needing complex graphics.
- **Diagonal color split on cover** — Abby Valencia Gooding uses teal as the dominant cover with pink diagonal slashes in corners. Creates dynamic movement and instantly signals a non-generic brand.
- **Geometric background pattern as texture** — Monica Guerra's cover uses a subtle line/circuit-board pattern on the green background. Adds depth without competing with the agent photo.
- **Property photo as background with color overlay** — Indy Park Place uses a horizontal line texture on charcoal background, with red brick accent block behind the agent. Minimal but distinctive.

#### Logo Design Patterns (new from this batch)
- **Overlapping initials monogram** — Abby Valencia Gooding's AVG monogram uses all three initials with the V bridging A and G — elegant, editorial, instantly memorable. The vertical pink bar separator between monogram and full name is a signature detail.
- **Overlapping KP mark** — Kenny Pleasant's KP mark overlaps K and P as large bold letterforms in orange — bold, confident, street-credible. Works at any size.
- **Abstract geometric icon as primary mark** — Monica Guerra's M mark is a geometric crown/gate shape built from the M initial — reads as luxury real estate without being literal about it.
- **Chevron/arrow house icon** — Steve Buzogany's logo uses stacked chevrons forming a house/spire shape — architectural, upward-momentum feeling, distinctive at thumbnail size.
- **House silhouette line art** — Indy Park Place uses a minimal house outline (line weight only, no fill) with a red window accent — clean, professional, market-ready.
- **Circular C mark** — Cat Cornish's brand mark is a bold circular C with internal cutout — works as app icon, profile picture, watermark. Shown in 4 background variations.
- **Script name as logo** — Christina Banhaal uses her written signature as the primary logo — intimate, personal, stands out against geometric logos everywhere else in real estate.

### Moodboard & Proposal Process Examples (Image 8)

This image reveals the professional design workflow used to create these brand systems. Critical knowledge for understanding what quality looks like at the proposal stage:

**The 3-Proposal System:**
Every brand development starts with a **moodboard** (inspiration images, color swatches, reference brands) then presents **3 distinct proposals** — each with:
1. Color palette + typography specimen
2. Logo mark variations (3–4 logo treatments)
3. Application example (social post mockup)

Agents choose one proposal, which then gets developed into the full style sheet.

**What moodboards include:**
- 8–12 inspiration images (other brands, product photography, lifestyle imagery, architecture)
- 3–5 color swatches extracted from the inspiration images
- Font specimen (display + body shown together)
- Mood words that connect visual direction to brand personality

**Design concepts seen in Image 8:**

*Adam Clark (PNW Dream Homes):*
- Moodboard: forest green, earth tones, adventure/Pacific Northwest imagery
- 3 proposals shown: #1 lion/crest mark (traditional), #2 tree/compass mark (PNW), #3 house mark (classic)
- Each proposal shown with matching social post mockup

*Abby Valencia:*
- Design concept titled "Refined Athletic Luxury — Color-Driven Identity"
- Written color rationale: Deep Green = stability/growth; Pink = modern elegance; Hot Pink = vibrant energy; White = purity
- Font rationale: Round Regular for headings (modern, condensed), Quasimord for body
- 3 proposals: minimalist monogram, serif wordmark, geometric mark

*Mindy Guevara:*
- Design concept titled "Professional and Warmth"
- Written color rationale for each swatch
- Font rationale explaining why each font was chosen
- 3 proposals with different mark approaches

*Franco Ippolito:*
- Moodboard: navy, gold, white — luxury professional
- Manrope font highlighted as primary
- 3 proposals with F-initial mark variations

**Key lesson from moodboards:** The best brand outputs start with written rationale — not just visual choices. Color meanings, font personality notes, and brand positioning statements are written out before a single logo is designed. When building a brand guide from scratch, this thinking should happen in Step 2 and be reflected in the brand voice section of the output.

### Updated Quality Standards (incorporating both batches)

**Cover/Hero must include:**
- [ ] Agent photo (prominent, professional quality assumed)
- [ ] Agent name (large — dominant typographic element)
- [ ] Market city explicitly stated
- [ ] Logo or monogram visible
- [ ] Background: dark with texture, gradient, skyline, or property photo overlay — never plain flat color
- [ ] At least one dynamic design element: diagonal slash, torn edge, geometric pattern, or color block

**Logo section must include:**
- [ ] Primary logo (mark + wordmark together)
- [ ] Mark/icon alone
- [ ] Light version (white on dark)
- [ ] Dark version (dark on light)
- [ ] At minimum 3, ideally 4 variations shown

**Color section must include:**
- [ ] Every swatch labeled with hex code
- [ ] Color name (brand-specific name, not generic — "Texas Rust" not "Brown")
- [ ] Usage rule per color
- [ ] 3–5 colors maximum

**Typography section must include:**
- [ ] Font name + weight explicitly stated
- [ ] Sample alphabet or text specimen shown
- [ ] Two fonts only (display + body) — exceptions only for label/accent fonts

**Graphic elements section must include:**
- [ ] 2–3 named elements (not just "pattern" — describe it: "dot halftone," "geometric line grid," "diagonal slash accent")
- [ ] How/where each element is used

**Mockups/Applications must include:**
- [ ] Minimum 2 applications
- [ ] At least one social media application (open house, just listed, or profile)
- [ ] At least one print application (business card) OR digital platform application (YouTube banner, email header)
- [ ] All mockups use real agent name, city, contact info — never placeholder text

**Brand differentiation test:**

## Batch 3 — Moodboard Deep Dive

This batch is exclusively moodboards — the pre-design stage before any logo or style sheet is created. This is the most important training layer for understanding HOW color palettes are derived and WHAT the visual direction looks like before a single design decision is made.

---

### What Moodboards Teach Us About Color Derivation

Every moodboard in this batch follows the same structure:
1. **8–15 inspiration images** — real brands, product photography, architecture, lifestyle imagery, material swatches, typography specimens
2. **5–6 color swatches extracted from those images** — shown as circles with hex codes
3. **A typography note** — "Typography plays a crucial role in conveying the brand's personality and positioning"
4. **A 1st logo draft** shown at the bottom (in some cases)

The critical insight: **colors come from imagery, not from the agent's preferences alone.** The designer selects images that match the agent's vibe, then extracts the palette from those images. This means:
- A "luxury masculine" brand → black matte product shots, gold hardware, leather textures → palette: black, dark charcoal, gold, warm tan, white
- A "warm feminine" brand → terracotta, dry grass, linen, sand → palette: cream, warm tan, blush, forest accent, near-black
- A "bold urban" brand → yellow construction, concrete, geometric architecture → palette: electric yellow, black, grey, white

When building a brand system, Claude should mentally "select inspiration images" for the agent's vibe and derive colors from what those images would contain — not just pick colors arbitrarily.

### Moodboard Construction Rules (for Claude to follow when building brand systems)

When Claude designs a brand system, it should mentally construct a moodboard first. Here's the process:

**Step 1: Identify the agent's brand personality archetype**

| Agent says... | Brand archetype | Imagery direction |
|---|---|---|
| "Luxury, premium, high-end" | Dark luxury | Black matte, gold foil, leather, marble, luxury packaging |
| "Warm, friendly, family" | Earthy warmth | Terracotta, linen, wood grain, dry grass, warm lifestyle |
| "Professional, established, trusted" | Classic professional | Navy leather, gold letterpress, architectural photography |
| "Bold, energetic, fun" | Urban bold | Yellow/black, concrete, streetwear brands, editorial |
| "Feminine, elegant, approachable" | Soft luxury | Blush, sage, cream, jewelry photography, interior design |
| "Modern, tech-forward, clean" | Contemporary minimal | White space, geometric patterns, sans-serif brands |
| "City expert, market authority" | Urban authority | City skyline, dark backgrounds, strong type, gold accent |
| "Nature, outdoor, PNW/mountain" | Organic premium | Forest green, wood, stone, architectural outdoor imagery |

**Step 2: Select imagery from that archetype**

Each archetype has signature imagery types:
- **Dark luxury:** Chanel-style flat lays, black business cards with gold foil, watch/jewelry product shots, dark marble, premium fragrance bottles
- **Earthy warmth:** Dry grass fields, terracotta pots, linen fabric, wood cutting boards, neutral interior design
- **Classic professional:** Navy leather portfolios, letterpress business cards, architectural facades, serif type specimens
- **Urban bold:** Yellow caution elements, concrete architecture, streetwear brand imagery, high-contrast editorial
- **Soft luxury:** Blush ceramics, sage plant photography, cream linen, jewelry flatlay, lifestyle photography
- **Contemporary minimal:** White space design, geometric brand systems, sans-serif pharmaceutical/tech brands, clean stationery

**Step 3: Extract the palette from the imagery**

From whatever imagery the archetype contains, extract:
- 1 near-black or very dark anchor (every palette needs this)
- 1 near-white or cream (every palette needs this)
- 1–2 mid-tones (the "brand colors" — what people think of when they think of the brand)
- 0–1 accent (optional pop color — used sparingly)

**Step 4: Name the colors with brand-specific names**

Never output "Dark Grey #3D3D3D" — always name it something tied to the brand:
- "Charcoal Foundation" / "Midnight Anchor" / "Deep Espresso" / "Ink Black"
- "Ivory Canvas" / "Warm Cream" / "Linen White" / "Porcelain"
- Name the brand colors after something evocative: "Texas Rust," "Pacific Sage," "Champagne Gold," "Ember Orange"

### Font + Style Moodboard Patterns

Every moodboard includes a "Font and style mood board" section. The note "Typography plays a crucial role in conveying the brand's personality and positioning" appears on every single one. This is not decorative — it's a reminder that font selection must be intentional.

Font selection by brand archetype:
- **Dark luxury:** Serif display (Cormorant, Playfair, Editorial New) + clean sans body. Script accent for signature elements.
- **Earthy warmth:** Warm serif (Libre Baskerville, Freight Display) + humanist sans (Source Sans, Lato)
- **Classic professional:** Classic serif (Garamond, Caslon) + geometric sans (Futura, Jost)
- **Urban bold:** Heavy condensed sans (Barlow Condensed, Impact, Bebas Neue) + modern sans body
- **Soft luxury:** Delicate serif (Cormorant Light, DM Serif) + rounded sans (Nunito, Rounded Mplus)
- **Contemporary minimal:** Geometric sans only, multiple weights (Montserrat, DM Sans, Outfit)
- **Urban authority:** Strong sans display (Josefin Sans, Syne, Neue Haas) + clean sans body
