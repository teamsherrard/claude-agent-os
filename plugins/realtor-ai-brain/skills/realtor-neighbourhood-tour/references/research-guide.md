# Research Guide — Neighbourhood Tour Skill

## What to Find and Why It Matters on Camera

The goal of research is to make the agent look like the most knowledgeable person
in the room — or in this case, the neighbourhood. Every stat, every restaurant name,
every school detail makes the video more credible and more useful to the buyer watching.

Viewers watching a neighbourhood tour video are considering moving there. They want
specific answers, not vague impressions. Research gives the agent those specific answers.

---

## Source Priority

### Real Estate Data
- Local real estate board website (CREB, TRREB, REBGV, etc.) — monthly stats
- Realtor.ca or Realtor.com — active listings and price ranges
- Zillow, Redfin — US neighbourhood data
- HouseSigma, Wahi — Canadian neighbourhood-level data
- Local news real estate sections — recent sales and trends

### Schools
- Fraser Institute School Rankings (Canada) — fraserinstitute.org
- GreatSchools (US) — greatschools.org
- Local school board websites — for catchment areas and programs
- School district websites — for French immersion, IB, specialty programs

### Amenities, Parks, Recreation
- Google Maps — search "[neighbourhood] parks" and "[neighbourhood] recreation"
- City of [city] parks and recreation website
- AllTrails — for trails and outdoor spaces
- Walk Score (walkscore.com) — walkability, transit, and bike scores

### Restaurants and Cafés
- Google Maps — "[neighbourhood] restaurants" sorted by rating
- Yelp — top rated in the neighbourhood
- Local food blogs and city guides
- TripAdvisor — neighbourhood-level search

### Development and Growth
- City planning department website — approved developments and rezoning
- Local news — search "[neighbourhood] development [year]"
- Real estate investment forums and reports

## What Makes a Stat "Camera-Ready"

Not every stat belongs on camera. A good camera-ready stat is:

1. **Specific** — "$680K average" not "prices are rising"
2. **Comparable** — "Up 8% from last year" not just the raw number
3. **Relevant to the target buyer** — a first-time buyer cares about entry price,
   not luxury market stats
4. **Easy to say out loud** — if it takes 15 seconds to explain the context, save it
   for the description instead
5. **Verifiable** — from a named source, not an estimate

### Good camera-ready stats:
- "Average detached home here is around $[X] — about 12% lower than [adjacent neighbourhood]"
- "[School name] is one of the top-rated elementary schools in the district"
- "Walk Score of 82 — you can get groceries, coffee, and the gym without a car"
- "3 new condo developments breaking ground here in [year] — this area is moving"

### Stats to avoid on camera:
- National or provincial averages (not specific enough)
- Stats older than 6 months without context
- Overly technical metrics buyers won't understand
- Anything that could be disputed or is hard to verify

## Matching Stats to Filming Stops

Each stop should have stats that make sense for that physical location.

| Stop Type | Stats to Pull |
|-----------|--------------|
| Park or green space | Walk Score, distance to amenities, trail length |
| School | School name, rating, programs, catchment boundary |
| Main street / commercial strip | Number of restaurants, walkability, transit score |
| New development | Number of units, completion date, price range |
| Residential street | Average home price, price trend, property types |
| Community centre | Programs, membership info, what it signals about the community |
| Coffee shop or restaurant | Name, what it's known for, what type of buyer this appeals to |

## Handling Research Gaps

Some neighbourhoods have limited publicly available data — especially smaller suburbs
or newer developments. When data isn't available:

1. Use the broader city data as context — "While [neighbourhood]-specific numbers
   aren't publicly tracked separately, homes in this part of [city] typically sell
   in the $[X]–$[X] range"
2. Use Walk Score and Google Maps data — always available
3. Use school board catchment maps — always available
4. Note the gap honestly in the cheat sheet rather than guessing

Never fabricate or estimate stats. If a specific number can't be found, use
qualitative observations from Google Maps reviews, local blogs, or the agent's
own knowledge if they provided context in the intake.
