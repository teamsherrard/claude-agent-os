# Script Guide — Drive-and-Talk Neighbourhood Tour Videos

## The Format

The agent films themselves driving through or walking around a neighbourhood.
They stop at 3-5 locations and talk about each one on camera. They transition
between stops while the camera is still rolling.

This is NOT a sit-down YouTube script. The agent is moving. They're pointing things
out. They're reacting to what they see. The script must match that energy — not a
teleprompter, but not completely improvised either.

**The hybrid approach:**
- **Word-for-word:** Hook and closing line only — these need to be crisp and consistent
- **Structured talking points:** Each stop has key lines, stats, and a suggested opener
- **Natural transitions:** Written to sound unscripted even though they're prepared

---

## The Hook — Word-for-Word Rules

The hook is the only fully scripted section beyond the closing line. It sets the
entire tone of the video. Get it right.

**What makes a great neighbourhood tour hook:**

Opens with the neighbourhood's single most compelling quality — not a greeting.
Names the neighbourhood in the first 5 seconds. Speaks directly to who this video
is for. Creates a reason to keep watching.

**Hook length:** 45-75 words. Under 30 seconds. Tight.

**Hook formulas by target buyer type:**

*For first-time buyers:*
"[Neighbourhood] in [City] is one of the most affordable areas where you can still
get a detached home under $[X]. I'm [Name] — real estate agent in [City] — and
today I'm walking you through [Neighbourhood] so you can see exactly what your
money gets you here."

*Schools-and-parks angle:*
"If you're moving to [City] and schools are at the top of your list — this video is
for you. [School name], [park name], [key amenity] — all within walking distance in
[Neighbourhood]. I'm [Name], and I'm taking you inside [Neighbourhood] today."

*For out-of-town relocators:*
"You've probably heard of [Neighbourhood] but have no idea what it actually feels like
to live here. I'm [Name] — local [City] agent — and today I'm going to show you
exactly what this community looks like from the inside."

*For downsizers:*
"[Neighbourhood] keeps coming up with my downsizer clients — and once you see it,
you'll understand why. Lock-and-leave lifestyle, walkable to everything, homes in
the $[X]–$[X] range. I'm [Name] — let me show you around."

## Stop Cards — Talking Points Format

Each stop is a filming moment, not a scripted monologue. The agent glances at
their talking points, then speaks naturally to camera.

**What every stop card needs:**

**What to show on camera** — specific visual direction. "Pan right to show the whole
park." "Get out of the car and walk toward the entrance." "Film from the corner so
viewers can see both the coffee strip and the residential street behind it."
This prevents the agent from just filming themselves talking with nothing behind them.

**Key talking points** — 3-4 bullets. Each one is a complete thought they can say
out loud. Mix of stats, observations, and buyer-relevant insights. Written in spoken
language — short sentences, natural rhythm.

BAD talking point: "Proximity to educational institutions and recreational facilities"
GOOD talking point: "Two elementary schools within a 5-minute walk — [Name] and [Name]"

BAD talking point: "Diverse culinary options available in the area"
GOOD talking point: "[Restaurant name] is right here — been there 15 years, always packed"

**Stats to mention** — the 1-2 numbers that fit this location. Pulled from research.
One line each. Specific.

**Suggested opening line** — one natural sentence they can use to start filming at
this stop. Not a script — a launching pad. Should sound like they just thought of it.

**Filming tip** — one practical direction for how to physically film this stop.

## Transition Lines — How to Write Them

Transitions happen while the agent is driving or walking to the next stop.
They're 10-20 seconds of connecting tissue between locations.

**What good transitions do:**
- Acknowledge what they just showed without over-summarizing
- Build anticipation for the next stop with a natural tease
- Sound like the agent is thinking out loud — not reading

**Transition formulas:**

*Reference + tease:*
"So that's the [what they just saw] — and honestly that's one of the things I always
point out to buyers here. Now we're heading to [next stop] which is about [distance/time]
away — and this is where it gets interesting."

*Contrast:*
"So [last stop] is the more [quality] side of [neighbourhood]. What I want to show you
next is completely different — this is where [next stop] comes in."

*Buyer bridge:*
"If you're [target buyer type], that last stop matters because [reason]. Now I'm going
to show you something that I think seals the deal for most people considering this area."

**Transition length:** 20-40 words. Under 15 seconds. If it's longer than that,
it becomes a second script and loses the natural feel.

## The Closing Line — Word-for-Word Rules

The final thing the agent says. Like the hook — word-for-word, clean, and specific.

**Closing line must include:**
1. One-sentence summary of why this neighbourhood is worth considering
2. Who it's best for — reference the target buyer type
3. The agent's CTA — specific and natural
4. A subscribe ask

**Closing formula:**
```
[Neighbourhood] is [one genuine, specific quality that sums it up].

If you're [target buyer type] looking in [City], this is one I'd put on your
shortlist — and I'd be happy to walk you through it in person.

[Agent's primary CTA from the Brain (identity/voice.md) — or: "Link in the description to
book a free call — we'll figure out if [Neighbourhood] is the right fit for you."]

And if you want more neighbourhood guides like this, subscribe —
I do one of these every single month.
```

## Voice Rules for Drive-and-Talk

**Sounds like:** A knowledgeable friend who lives nearby showing you around.
Relaxed, specific, genuinely enthusiastic about what they're showing without
being salesy. Like they've walked these streets a hundred times.

**Never sounds like:**
- A real estate ad ("this desirable family-friendly community offers...")
- A tourist brochure ("nestled in the heart of...")
- A news report ("the area has seen significant growth in recent years...")
- A salesperson ("this is a really great opportunity for buyers...")

**Specific language rules:**
- Name actual streets, businesses, parks, and schools — never "the local café" or "nearby amenities"
- Use "you" constantly — the viewer is mentally walking with the agent
- Make observations, not pitches — "this park gets packed on weekends" not "great green space"
- Be honest about trade-offs — if parking is tough or the main road is busy, say so.
  Honesty builds more trust than a perfect pitch.
