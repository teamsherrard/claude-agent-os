# Listing Research Guide — Neighbourhood and Market Context

## Why Research Matters for Listing Content

A listing video that only talks about the property itself misses half the sale.
Buyers aren't just evaluating the home — they're evaluating the neighbourhood,
the schools, the commute, the lifestyle. Research gives the agent the ammunition
to sell both.

Every stat and named location pulled from research makes the content more credible,
more shareable, and more useful to the exact buyer this home is for.

---

## What to Find

### Priority 1 — Price Context
Find the neighbourhood average or benchmark price so the agent can frame the
asking price intelligently on camera.

"At $[X], this home is [above/below/at] the neighbourhood average of $[Y] —
and here's what you get for that."

This framing is far more powerful than just stating the price. It gives context.

Sources:
- Local real estate board stats pages
- Realtor.ca or Realtor.com neighbourhood data
- HouseSigma or Wahi (Canada)
- Zillow or Redfin neighbourhood pages (US)

### Priority 2 — Schools
Always find the actual school names in the catchment area.
"Good schools nearby" is useless. "[School name] elementary — one of the top-rated
in the district" is what buyers remember.

Sources:
- Local school board website — catchment map by address
- Fraser Institute (Canada) — fraserinstitute.org/school-performance
- GreatSchools (US) — greatschools.org
- Google: "[address] school catchment [city]"

### Priority 3 — Named Amenities
Find 2-3 specific named locations near the property worth mentioning.
Not "restaurants nearby." "[Restaurant name] — been there 20 years, always packed."
Not "parks in the area." "[Park name] — 5 acres, off-leash dog area, splash pad."

Sources:
- Google Maps — search restaurants, parks, coffee within 1km/0.5mi
- Walk Score — walkscore.com (also provides transit and bike scores)
- City parks and recreation website

### Priority 4 — Commute and Transit
How long does it take to get downtown or to major employment centres?
Is there LRT, bus, or major highway access?

Sources:
- Google Maps — driving and transit time from address to downtown
- Transit authority route maps
- Walk Score transit score

### Priority 5 — Any Development or Growth Story
Is anything new happening nearby? A new school, a park expansion, a commercial
development, an LRT extension? This adds a forward-looking angle to the content.

Sources:
- City planning department website
- Local news — search "[neighbourhood] development [year]"

---

## Price Framing Guide

Use this to frame the asking price intelligently based on what research shows:

| Asking price vs neighbourhood avg | How to frame it |
|---|---|
| 5-10% below average | "Priced below the neighbourhood average — rare in this area right now" |
| At average | "Priced right at market — comparable to similar homes here" |
| 5-10% above average | "Premium pricing for the upgrades and features — here's what justifies it" |
| Significantly above | Focus on unique features and lifestyle — let the content justify the price |
| Significantly below | Lead with the value angle — "one of the best-priced homes in [neighbourhood] right now" |

---

## What NOT to Research

- National or provincial averages — too broad to be meaningful for a specific listing
- Stats older than 3 months — stale data undermines credibility
- Anything that can't be verified with a named source

---

## Research Gaps

If neighbourhood-specific price data isn't publicly available:
Use the broader city or quadrant data with context —
"While [neighbourhood]-specific stats aren't tracked separately, homes in this
part of [city] typically sell in the $[X]–$[X] range, and this listing sits at..."

If school catchment can't be confirmed:
Note "confirm catchment schools with the local school board" rather than guessing.
Getting a school catchment wrong in a video is a credibility killer.
